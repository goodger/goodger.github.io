Saku, Nagano

We chose to live in Saku because of its relatively cool (or at least bearable) summers due to its location in the mountains (elevation 760 m ≈ 2500 ft). In exchange, we experience cold winters. Because ours is an old house, when it’s cold outside it’s cold inside as well.

Saku is a city of approximately 100,000 residents spread out over 423 km² in the eastern part of central Nagano Prefecture. Saku was formed by the agglomeration of multiple towns (Iwamurada, Nakagomi, Nozawa, Usuda, Mochizuki, Asashina, Asama) which remain distinct neighborhoods with their own downtowns. Much of Saku remains agricultural. Our house is in a residential neighborhood in the northeast part of Saku with shopping nearby, but farm fields, woods, and the Yu River are only a few minutes walk away.

Saku contains the point furthest from the sea within Honshū island and is surrounded by mountains: the Asama Range to the north, Mount Hirao to the east, and Mount Tatéshina & the Yatsugatake Mountain Range to the south. Saku has relatively little precipitation and many sunny days thanks to these mountains. Saku has hot and humid summers and relatively mild winters. Mount Asama is a volcano, mildly active for now; the last major eruption was over 200 years ago.

In 1997 the Hokuriku Shinkansen bullet train line (formerly the Nagano Shinkansen) opened with Sakudaira station in Saku. From Sakudaira Station it takes 70-85 minutes to Tokyo Station (some trains skip smaller stations). Many people now commute to Tokyo by bullet train. Saku is also connected to surrounding areas by local trains. The area around Sakudaira station has become the downtown area of greater Saku, with a shopping center, large stores, many restaurants, and a community park.

Immediately to the east of Saku is Gunma Prefecture. To the north are the towns of Komoro (population 42,000), Miyota (15,000), and Karuizawa (a famous resort town where wealthy people have summer homes; population 20,000). To the west is the town of Tateshina (7100). Further to the west of Saku are the cities of Ueda (157,000), Nagano (the capital of Nagano Prefecture; population 365,000), and Matsumoto (239,000).

Our Home

As a guest in our home you will experience staying in a real late-Shōwa-era Japanese house, built in 1977. The experience will be similar to staying at a minshuku:

Minshuku are a low-budget version of ryokan, roughly equivalent to a British boarding house or a bed and breakfast. The facilities... may simply consist of spare rooms in a family home. Minshuku often serve as the only type of accommodation in towns or villages too small to warrant a dedicated hotel or ryokan. The overall experience is much the same, but the food is simpler, dining may be optional and is often communal, rooms don’t usually have a private toilet, and guests may have to lay out their own bedding.

—Wikipedia

Ours is a big house for two people by Japanese standards, but modest by Canadian standards: 177 m² (≈ 1900 sq.ft) including storage spaces, hallways, and even the thickness of the walls. Like most houses in Japan, there’s no basement. When we bought the house in 2024 it had been empty for 5 years, one of Japan’s many akiya (empty houses). When it was built our house replaced a much older and larger house (“minka”). The family who used to live here were coincidentally also named Takahashi (it’s the third most common family name in Japan) and many relatives live in the area, some nearby. The Takahashi family were local farmers, and many farming tools and supplies came with the house.

Our property’s land area is 530 m² ≈ 5700 sq.ft., mostly filled by the house and 4 outbuildings: a 2-car garage, a 2-storey grain storehouse, a small shed in front of the house, and a large shed in the back. We plan to remove the large shed in the back, freeing up space for a yard or for a vegetable garden, and the small shed in the front, freeing up space for a deck or for a small yard or for a traditional Japanese decorative garden. There’s a small empty property to the east (211 m² ≈ 2270 sq.ft), and other akiya border our property to the north and west and elswhere in the neighborhood.

Our house is adjacent to the historic Old Nakasendō (Central Mountain Route), “one of the centrally administered five walking routes of the Edo period, and one of the two that connected the de facto capital of Japan at Edo (modern-day Tokyo) to Kyoto [the old capital]”, “official routes for the use of the Shōgun and the other Daimyō [feudal lords]” [—Wikipedia]. Our property is near Otai-shuku, one of the “post towns” (#21) or rest areas where people would stop on their journeys. For a personal pilgrimage/challenge many people still walk the 534 km route between Tōkyō and Kyōto, which takes about 3 weeks. About a week after leaving Tōkyō they pass right by our house!

Details:

  • The front entrance or “genkan” features a step up to the main floor. We always take off and leave our outside shoes here, step up, and (optionally) put on slippers. If your feet are larger than US men’s size 8, you may want to bring your own slippers.

    In most houses when you enter a tatami-floored washitsu, you take off your slippers and wear socks or bare feet. This is to avoid scuffing the delicate tatami surface. We don’t observe this custom in our house; we just try to keep our floors and slippers clean. The tatami mats are old and we’re treating them as ordinary flooring.

    It’s also common to take off your slippers and put on special slippers/sandals when entering a toilet room or the bathroom. We don’t observe this custom either.

  • Our guest rooms on the ground floor are “washitsu”: Japanese-style tatami-floored multifuntion rooms with Western-style beds. When we don’t have guests, they are our formal living rooms and overflow space.

    These rooms are separated by sliding doors. Some are fusuma (cloth-covered opaque sliding doors) and some are shōji (sliding wood-framed doors with translucent washi paper screens and glass panels). All are pretty thin: not much sound insulation.

  • Doorways into the kitchen and into all of the washitsu are low, about 175 cm (5'9"). If you’re taller than this (like David) be careful to duck or you’ll hit your head (like David does occasionally; ouch!). Other doorways are reasonably high.

  • In the northeast corner of the house is our kitchen, which you’re welcome to use: refrigerator, microwave/grill combination oven, gas range, toaster oven, etc. We’re always well stocked with snacks, both Japanese & Western.

  • South of the kitchen is our great room:

    • The dining room where we’ll share meals is another washitsu, but with a Western dining table and chairs.
    • The media room, adjacent to the dining room on the south side, is Western-style (wood flooring), and contains our extensive media collection, which you’re welcome to use.

    In 2025 we took down the wall between the dining room and the media room, added a wood pellet stove, and created our combined “great room”.

  • There’s only a single bathroom (the room with the bath). See How to Use a Japanese Bath. We provide towels, soap, and shampoo.

    There are onsen hot spring baths nearby. A recommended experience!

  • The anteroom to the bathroom has a washbasin and is also our laundry room with a washing machine and a dryer (a rare luxury in Japan). You’re welcome to use it!

  • The toilets are in their own separate small/tiny rooms, true “water closets”. We have one toilet on the ground floor, and a second toilet upstairs — rare for a house this old!

  • There’s no central heating or air conditioning. We only heat and cool the rooms we’re actually using. We have several individual air conditioner / heat pump units for cooling. For heaters we have a pellet stove in the great room and “fan heaters” (natural gas & kerosene) for individual rooms. We wear sweaters and warm slippers during cold weather.

Ideas For Dining In

  • Onabé is a chunky soup/stew with a variety of broths and ingredients, great in cold weather.
  • Barbecue: we inherited years worth of wood charcoal (not briquettes) along with the house! Burgers, chicken, fish, etc.
  • A typical téishoku or “set meal” includes rice, miso soup, and a salad. This is in addition to the okazu, the featured entrée, like fish or meat or sautéed vegetables.

Destinations In & Near Saku

We have visited the places marked with a “★” and can recommend them. Many of the others are on our to-visit list.

Walking Courses

  • Walk the Old Nakasendō road to or from:

    • ★ Otai-shuku, a small Nakasendō post town (#21) a short walk away to the north (10-15 minutes, ~800 m)
    • Oiwaké-shuku is the next post town (#20) toward Tōkyō (e.g. the Sherlock Holmes statue; about 95 minutes one-way, ~6.1 km)
    • Karuizawa (e.g. Old Karuizawa Ginza Street in Karuisawa-shuku [#18]; about 4 hours one-way, ~16 km), or
    • ★ Kumanokotai Shrine on the border with Gunma Prefecture (about 5 hours one-way, ~19 km). The route becomes a trail through the woods east of here. There’s a café across the street, Shigenoya, with a deck overlooking the mountain slope to the south, and late-blooming cherry trees (2025-04-25).
    • Iwamurada-shuku is the next post town (#22) to the south of us, towards Kyōto (e.g. Sainenji temple, about 50 minutes one-way, 3.6 km)

    For the distant destinations, perhaps drive there and walk home or arrange to meet there and drive home. Or drive there and walk around the area.

  • Walking courses around Saku. (★ We’ve walked a couple of these.)

  • ★ Walk the back road between Route 9 and Old Nakasendō to Nagakura Suwa Shrine, turn right and walk to Hōshu-in, past the water treatment plant, to Miyota Fujitsuka Reien cemetery to take in the view of Mount Hirao and the Shinkansen tracks, then back home (~2.7 km).

Nature

  • ★ Cosmos plantation. The view from the top of the hill is impressive.
  • ★ Iwamurada Schistostega is a cave by the Yu River containing luminescent moss.
  • ★ There’s a small forested gorge 10 minutes walk from our house, on the way to the Yu River valley.

Mountain Climbing/Trekking/Hiking

  • Mount Hirao
  • Many others; we’re surrounded by mountains.

Historic Sites

  • ★ Tatsuoka-jō Castle Ruins (star fort) in Saku. The ruins themselves are not impressive; the original buildings are gone, and a now-closed elementary school is all that’s left. There’s a visitor center there with a mockup and historical information.
  • ★ Portions of the Komoro Castle remain and are quite impressive.
  • ★ Ueda Castle Ninomaru Ruins. Rebuilt hilltop remnants of a castle dating to 1583, with 2 Shinto shrines & a local history museum.

Onsen Hot Spring Baths

  • ★ Hirao Onsen Miharashinoyu is a large-scale place nearby, about 10 minutes drive to the south. There are multiple outdoor baths, including individual “pots”. Beyond the privacy fencing there are panoramic views of the mountains surrounding Saku to the east, south, and west. On clear days you can see all the way to the North Japanese Alps.
  • ★ Fusé Onsen, about 20 minutes drive to the west, is a smaller place on a hillside overlooking farm fields in a river valley.
  • ★ Tatéshina Onsen Gongen-no-yu, about 30 minutes to the west, is a medium-scale place. Its main attraction is its outdoor bath, with a spectacular view of the mountains of the Asama Range to the north. If you visit only one onsen, this is the one.

Buddhist Temples

  • ★ Hōshuin, a short walk away, features a small pond with koi fish, a bamboo forest, and a view of a creek at the bottom of a neighboring ravine.

  • ★ Saku-Naritasan-Yakushiji features Pin-koro jizō, named from the phrase “pinpin-korori”: to live a long and healthy life, and to die peacefully without becoming bedridden (info).

    A small “Sanmon Market” is held here on the second Saturday of every month. There’s some parking there, or we can take the train to Nakagomi Station and walk.

  • Sainenji (Jōdo Sect Buddhist temple), in town behind the Iwamurada branch of Hachijūni (82) Bank.

  • Shuhoin on Mount Hirao, near Hirao Shrine.

  • ★ Kogakuji & many other nearby templess in Komoro.

Shintō Shrines

  • ★ Shirayamahimé Shrine, a small Otai neighborhood shrine on our block.
  • ★ Inari Shrine, a tiny shrine in the Otai neighborhood, two blocks from our house.
  • ★ Nagakura Suwa Shrine is a medium-sized shrine 15 minutes walk from our house.
  • ★ Iidama Shrine is a medium-sized shrine 1.5 km north of our house, a 22 minute walk. It’s in the woods up a flight of steps from the back road west of the Old Nakasendō road, near Bakery Café Cocoladé.
  • Hirao Shrine, near Shuhoin Buddhist temple on Mount Hirao. 10 minutes by car.
  • ★ Hanazura-Inari Shrine is high on a hillside overlooking the Yu River and has a staircase with a series of red torii gates, plus a bamboo forest and Japanese maples. 10 minutes by car.
  • ★ Shinkai-Sansha Shrine is a large shrine in a gorgeous setting: in the woods on a hillside. 25 minutes by car.
  • Inariyama Park has a shrine with lots of torii gates, plus castle ruins and a rocket-shaped “Cosmo Tower”. 25 minutes by car.

Mountain Roads

  • ★ Cosmos Highroad (コスモス街道; National Highway 254 east out of Saku) & Uchiyama Gorge (内山峡). In late summer it’s lined with cosmos flowers.
  • The winding roads between Saku and Takasaki have some breathtaking scenery. Highway 18 is pretty good, but Highway 254 is even better (though longer; it starts as the Cosmos Highroad).

Museums

  • Saku Municipal Museum of Modern Art in Komaba Park. There’s also a library and a public pool. Near the apartment we initially rented. 12 minutes by car.
  • Trick Art Museum Karuizawa
  • ★ Karuizawa Picturebook Museum
  • Peynet Museum (modern art), Karuizawa
  • Hiroshi Senju Museum Karuizawa (modern art)

Festivals

“Matsuri” (祭) in Japanese.

  • Saku hot-air balloon festival in May.
  • Neighborhood festivals here & there throughout the summer.

Go

  • ★ The Komoro Go Club meets on Saturdays at 13:00, with lessons for beginners at 09:00 (~10 km, 20 minutes by car).
  • The Ueda Go Hall (Nihon Ki-in Ueda Branch) is open daily (~33 km, 40 minutes by car).
  • Groups (of mostly retired men) meet weekly at community centers in and around Saku:
    • ★ Wednesdays 09:00–16:00 at Asama Kaikan (3.6 km)
    • Fridays 10:00~ at Saku City General Welfare Center, 249-2 Sarukubo (長壽閣/Chōjukaku; 5.9 km)
    • Saturdays 13:00~ at Nagatoro Community Center (4.8 km)
  • ★ Second-hand stores often carry Go boards, stones, and bowls. Nagano Recycle Center (walking distance) has several floor boards with legs.

Miscellaneous

  • ★ Otai Mallet Golf course; 750 m southeast. (We haven’t played yet.)
  • Karuizawa Taliesin is a private nature park with a lake, museums, restaurants, and activities.
  • ★ Mount Hirao Parada has skiing in winter and a “Super Slider” sled course in summer, plus an insectarium (featuring a stag beetle dome) any time. Also, hiking through the woods. And an onsen, of course.
  • ★ Ryūjin’s Forest Park in Miyota is a municipal park 2.5 km north via the Old Nakasendō road etc.

Shopping

  • ★ Home centers: Viva Home and Cainz.
